The Ultimate Packing List for Long-Term Travel (That Fits in a Carry-On)

  • Post author By Mixtry14
  • Post date 5 April 2026
  • Categories In Uncategorized

Pack less than you think you need, and you’ll travel better than you ever have. Here’s the complete packing list for long-term travel, all carry-on size.

buzzfeed.com

The single most common mistake first-time long-term travellers make is packing too much. You will use 20% of what you bring 80% of the time. Everything else is dead weight.

Here’s a packing list refined over years of long-term travel. All of it fits in a 40L carry-on bag.

The Golden Rules of Packing

  1. Lay everything out. Then put half of it back.
  2. If you’re unsure whether to bring something, don’t bring it.
  3. You can buy almost anything you forget, almost anywhere in the world — often cheaper than at home.
  4. Wear your heaviest items on travel days (boots, jacket).

Clothing

The goal is a mix-and-match capsule wardrobe in neutral colours that all work together.

For warm climates:

  • 3 × moisture-wicking t-shirts
  • 1 × long-sleeve shirt (sun protection + cooler evenings)
  • 2 × pairs of shorts
  • 1 × lightweight trousers (versatile — good for temples, nicer restaurants, cooler days)
  • 1 × swimsuit
  • 5 × pairs of underwear (merino wool if budget allows — resists odour, dries fast)
  • 3 × pairs of socks
  • 1 × light rain jacket (doubles as a wind layer)
  • 1 × lightweight fleece or hoodie

Footwear:

  • 1 × pair of trail runners or hiking shoes (wear these on travel days)
  • 1 × pair of sandals (Chacos or Tevas hold up far better than flip flops)

Toiletries

  • Solid shampoo bar (no liquid restrictions, lasts longer)
  • Solid conditioner bar
  • Bar soap or body wash
  • Toothbrush + toothpaste tablets
  • Deodorant
  • Razor
  • Sunscreen (small, top up locally)
  • Lip balm with SPF
  • Small first aid kit (plasters, blister pads, pain relief, antidiarrheal, antihistamine)
  • Prescription medications (bring more than you think you need)

Electronics

  • Smartphone
  • Lightweight laptop or tablet (if needed for work)
  • Universal power adapter
  • Portable power bank (10,000mAh)
  • USB charging cables
  • Earbuds or headphones
  • E-reader (optional, but transforms long travel days)

Documents & Money

  • Passport (valid for at least 6 months beyond travel dates)
  • Physical copies of key documents in a separate bag
  • Travel insurance card and policy number
  • 2 × bank cards from different accounts (in case one is blocked)
  • Small amount of local currency for arrival

Pack & Organization

  • 40L travel backpack (Osprey Farpoint 40 or similar)
  • 2–3 × packing cubes (compress clothing, keep bag organized)
  • Small daypack or packable tote (for day trips — don’t carry your main bag everywhere)
  • Dry bag (protects electronics in rain or on water activities)
  • Padlock (for hostel lockers)

What People Wish They’d Left at Home

  • More than one pair of jeans (heavy, slow to dry, take up huge space)
  • A full-size towel (most hostels provide them; a quick-dry travel towel is fine)
  • Too many shoes
  • Books (e-reader or local book exchanges)
  • Excessive toiletries (buy locally)

The moment you hoist a light pack onto your back and walk easily through an airport while others struggle with check-in luggage — you’ll understand why packing light is worth the effort.

Solo Travel Safety Tips Every Adventurer Should Know

  • Post author By Mixtry14
  • Post date 5 April 2026
  • Categories In Uncategorized

Solo travel is one of life’s most rewarding experiences , and with the right preparation, it’s far safer than most people think. Here’s how to travel alone with confidence.

thetravelwomen.com

When you tell people you’re travelling solo, you’ll get one of two reactions: envy or worry. Usually both, from the same person.

The truth is that solo travel is safer than it looks from the outside — and more life-changing than it looks from anywhere. Here’s how to do it right.

Before You Leave

Research your destination honestly

Don’t rely on either extreme — the friend who says everywhere is dangerous, or the travel blogger who says everywhere is fine. Use:

  • Government travel advisories (Canada.ca/travel for Canadians) for factual risk assessments
  • Travel forums like Reddit’s r/solotravel for recent first-hand accounts
  • Local Facebook groups for destination-specific up-to-date information

Get comprehensive travel insurance

This is non-negotiable. Medical evacuation from a remote area can cost tens of thousands of dollars. A good travel insurance policy costs a fraction of that.

Look for coverage that includes: medical, emergency evacuation, trip cancellation, and gear theft. Compare options at InsureMyTrip or CAA Travel Insurance.

Make copies of important documents

  • Passport photo page
  • Travel insurance policy
  • Accommodation confirmations
  • Emergency contacts

Email copies to yourself and a trusted person at home. Store physical copies separately from the originals.

Share your itinerary

Tell someone at home where you’re going and check in regularly. You don’t need to report your every move — just a quick message every day or two so someone knows you’re okay.

On the Ground

Trust your instincts

Your gut is a finely tuned safety instrument. If a situation, a person, or a place feels wrong — leave. You don’t owe anyone an explanation. The small awkwardness of walking away from something uncomfortable is nothing compared to the alternative.

Stay aware of your surroundings

  • Keep your phone put away when walking in unfamiliar areas
  • Be aware of who is near you, especially at night
  • Walk with purpose and confidence even when you’re lost

Protect your valuables

  • Use a money belt or hidden pouch for your passport and cards
  • Carry only the cash you need for the day
  • Use the hostel or hotel safe for your passport and extra cash
  • Never leave your bag unattended

Be careful with alcohol

This is one of the most consistent factors in travel incidents. Know your limits, keep an eye on your drink, and get home safely. A great rule: stay sober enough to make good decisions.

Connect with other travellers

Solo doesn’t have to mean alone. Hostels, group tours, and travel apps like Meetup or Couchsurfing events are great for meeting people. Travel together for a day or a week — then go your separate ways. This is one of the best parts of solo travel.

Digital Safety

  • Use a VPN when connecting to public WiFi
  • Enable two-factor authentication on your banking and email
  • Be careful what you post publicly on social media while travelling — don’t advertise your exact location in real time

If Something Goes Wrong

  • Medical emergency: Know the local emergency number (not always 911). Your travel insurance card should have a 24/7 helpline.
  • Theft: File a police report immediately — you’ll need it for insurance claims. Contact your bank to freeze cards.
  • Lost passport: Contact your nearest Canadian embassy or consulate.

The best solo travellers aren’t fearless — they’re prepared. Preparation is what lets you be bold, because you’ve already thought through the what-ifs.

Best Hiking Gear for Beginners: What You Actually Need (And What You Don’t)

  • Post author By Mixtry14
  • Post date 5 April 2026
  • Categories In Uncategorized

Don’t let gear overwhelm you before you even hit the trail. Here’s the honest beginner’s guide to hiking equipment — what’s essential, what’s nice to have, and what’s a waste of money.

rei.com

Walk into any outdoor gear store as a beginner and you’ll walk out either overwhelmed, over-budget, or both. The gear industry is very good at convincing you that you need everything. You don’t.

Here’s what you actually need to start hiking safely and comfortably.

The Essentials (Don’t Skip These)

1. Hiking Boots or Trail Shoes

Your most important piece of kit. Blisters and ankle injuries end trips — good footwear prevents both.

  • Trail runners (low-cut, lightweight) are great for well-maintained trails in dry conditions
  • Hiking boots (mid or high-cut) offer more ankle support and waterproofing for rougher terrain
  • Whatever you choose: break them in before your trip. Never hike long distances in new footwear.

Budget pick: Merrell Moab 3 (~$150 CAD) Upgrade pick: Salomon X Ultra 4 (~$220 CAD)

2. Moisture-Wicking Base Layer

Cotton kills — it holds sweat and moisture against your skin, leading to chafing and in cold conditions, hypothermia. Invest in a synthetic or merino wool base layer.

Budget pick: Any synthetic hiking shirt (~$30–50 CAD) Upgrade pick: Icebreaker Merino Wool T-Shirt (~$90 CAD)

3. A Reliable Daypack

For day hikes, a 20–30L pack is ideal. Look for padded shoulder straps, a hip belt (transfers weight off your shoulders), and a hydration sleeve.

Budget pick: Osprey Daylite (~$75 CAD) Upgrade pick: Osprey Talon 22 (~$160 CAD)

4. Water and a Way to Filter It

Dehydration is the number one cause of problems on trail. Carry at least 2L of water and know how to get more.

  • A hydration bladder (like a Camelbak) keeps water accessible without stopping
  • A water filter like the Sawyer Squeeze ($50 CAD) means you can drink from streams safely

5. Navigation

Don’t rely solely on your phone’s battery. Download offline maps on Maps.me or AllTrails before you leave, and consider carrying a paper map for longer hikes.

6. The Ten Essentials

These should be in your pack on every hike:

  1. Navigation (map + compass or GPS)
  2. Sun protection (sunscreen, sunglasses, hat)
  3. Insulation (extra layer)
  4. Illumination (headlamp + spare batteries)
  5. First-aid kit
  6. Fire (lighter or matches)
  7. Repair tools and knife
  8. Nutrition (extra food)
  9. Hydration (extra water)
  10. Emergency shelter (emergency bivy or space blanket)

Nice to Have

  • Trekking poles — massively reduce knee strain on descents, especially with a heavy pack
  • Gaiters — keep debris and water out of your boots in rough terrain
  • Buff/neck gaiter — versatile, lightweight, blocks sun and wind

What You Don’t Need Yet

  • A $600 GPS device (your phone with offline maps is fine to start)
  • Specialist mountaineering gear (unless you’re mountaineering)
  • Every single layer in an outdoor retailer’s catalogue

Start simple, hike often, and let your gear evolve with your experience. Most seasoned hikers will tell you their kit has actually gotten smaller over the years, not bigger.

How to Plan a Budget Backpacking Trip: The Complete Beginner’s Guide

  • Post author By Mixtry14
  • Post date 5 April 2026
  • Categories In Uncategorized

Think you can’t afford to travel? Think again. Here’s exactly how to plan an incredible backpacking trip without breaking the bank.

novo-monde.com

The biggest lie in travel is that it’s expensive. Yes, it can be. But it doesn’t have to be — and for millions of backpackers every year, it isn’t.

With the right approach, you can travel for weeks or months on a budget that would surprise you. Here’s exactly how to do it.

Step 1: Choose a Budget-Friendly Region

Your destination choice is the single biggest factor in your daily costs. Some regions are dramatically cheaper than others.

Most affordable regions for backpackers:

  • Southeast Asia (Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, Indonesia)
  • Central America (Guatemala, Nicaragua, Honduras)
  • Eastern Europe (Albania, North Macedonia, Georgia, Moldova)
  • Central Asia (Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan)
  • South America (Bolivia, Peru, Colombia)

In Southeast Asia, for example, a comfortable backpacker lifestyle — dorm or cheap private room, street food, local transport, activities — can cost as little as $30–$50 CAD per day.

Step 2: Book Flights Strategically

Flights are often the biggest single expense. Here’s how to keep costs down:

  • Be flexible with dates. Flying mid-week is almost always cheaper than weekends.
  • Use Google Flights to browse an entire month’s prices at once with the calendar view.
  • Set fare alerts on Google Flights or Hopper for your route.
  • Consider budget airlines for regional hops — AirAsia in Southeast Asia, Wizz Air in Europe.
  • Fly into major hubs then use ground transport to reach your destination. Flying to Bangkok, then bussing to Chiang Mai, is almost always cheaper than flying direct to Chiang Mai.

Step 3: Accommodation on a Budget

Hostels are your best friend. A dorm bed in a good hostel costs a fraction of a hotel room, and you’ll meet fellow travellers instantly.

Tips for hostel stays:

  • Read reviews carefully on Hostelworld or Booking.com
  • Look for hostels with free breakfast — it adds up
  • A 6-bed dorm is usually the sweet spot between price and privacy
  • Don’t dismiss private rooms in hostels — they’re often cheaper than budget hotels

Other affordable options:

  • Couchsurfing — stay with locals for free (great for cultural exchange)
  • Guesthouses — family-run, often excellent value, especially in Asia
  • Work exchanges on Workaway or WWOOF — free room and board in exchange for a few hours of work daily

Step 4: Eat Like a Local

Food is one of the easiest places to save — and eating local is almost always the better experience anyway.

  • Eat where locals eat. If there’s no local language on the menu, keep walking.
  • Street food is safe, delicious, and incredibly cheap in most of the world.
  • Cook occasionally. Most hostels have a kitchen. A supermarket run can save you a lot over a week.
  • Avoid tourist areas for meals. Walk two streets back from the main square and prices often halve.

Step 5: Get Around Cheaply

  • Local buses and trains over taxis and tourist shuttles wherever possible
  • Overnight buses or trains save you a night of accommodation costs
  • Walk — the best way to discover a city is on foot, and it’s free
  • Rent a motorbike in Southeast Asia — cheap, flexible, and a great experience (get insurance and a licence)

Step 6: Build Your Daily Budget

Here’s a realistic daily budget breakdown for Southeast Asia:

A 30-day trip at $45/day = $1,350 CAD on the ground, plus your flights.

Step 7: The Costs People Forget

Budget for these before you leave:

  • Travel insurance (non-negotiable — get it)
  • Visas
  • Vaccinations
  • Gear and equipment
  • Airport transport at home
  • First and last night accommodation (often pricier as you sort yourself out)

The truth about budget travel? It’s less about sacrifice and more about priorities. You give up the fancy hotel and the tourist restaurants — and in return you get real experiences, real connections, and the freedom to stay as long as you want.

10 Underrated Adventure Destinations You Need to Visit Before Everyone Else Does.

  • Post author By Mixtry14
  • Post date 5 April 2026
  • Categories In Uncategorized

boredpanda.com

Skip the crowds. These 10 destinations offer world-class adventure, stunning landscapes, and authentic local culture, without the tourist prices.

You’ve seen the photos of Santorini. You know about Bali. But if you’re the kind of traveller who’d rather discover a place than just visit it, this list is for you.

These are destinations that deliver extraordinary experiences — jaw-dropping scenery, rich culture, incredible food — while still feeling like a genuine discovery.

1. Faroe Islands, Denmark

Sitting in the North Atlantic between Norway and Iceland, the Faroe Islands look like a fantasy painting come to life. Dramatic cliffs, waterfalls that spill directly into the ocean, and villages perched on hillsides — all with almost no crowds.

Best for: Hiking, photography, dramatic coastal scenery Best time to visit: June to August Getting there: Fly via Copenhagen or Reykjavik

2. Kyrgyzstan, Central Asia

One of the most underrated adventure destinations on earth. Kyrgyzstan offers world-class trekking through the Tian Shan mountains, horseback riding across open steppe, and the chance to sleep in a traditional yurt with a nomadic family.

Best for: Trekking, horseback riding, cultural immersion Best time to visit: May to October Getting there: Fly to Bishkek via Istanbul or Moscow

3. Azores, Portugal

Nine volcanic islands in the middle of the Atlantic, the Azores offer whale watching, crater lakes, hot springs, and dramatic coastlines — all within the EU and more affordable than mainland Portugal.

Best for: Whale watching, hiking, diving Best time to visit: April to October Getting there: Direct flights from Lisbon, London, and several US cities

4. Oman

While its neighbours dominate the headlines, Oman quietly offers some of the Middle East’s best adventure travel. Desert dunes, dramatic wadis, ancient forts, and some of the friendliest locals you’ll ever meet.

Best for: Desert camping, hiking, cultural history Best time to visit: October to April Getting there: Fly to Muscat from most major hubs

5. Tajikistan

The Pamir Highway is one of the greatest road trips on Earth, cutting through some of the planet’s most remote and spectacular mountain landscapes. Tourism is still minimal, which means prices are low and experiences are authentic.

Best for: Road trips, trekking, off-the-grid exploration Best time to visit: June to September Getting there: Fly to Dushanbe

6. Albania

Europe’s secret is finally starting to get out — but Albania still offers dramatic mountain ranges, a stunning Adriatic and Ionian coastline, and UNESCO-listed cities at a fraction of the cost of neighbouring Greece or Croatia.

Best for: Hiking, beaches, budget travel Best time to visit: May to September Getting there: Fly to Tirana from most European hubs

7. Namibia

Africa’s most undervisited country is also one of its most spectacular. The Namib Desert is the oldest on earth, and the landscapes — from red sand dunes to desert-adapted wildlife — are unlike anywhere else.

Best for: Wildlife, photography, self-drive adventure Best time to visit: May to October Getting there: Fly to Windhoek

8. North Macedonia

Landlocked between its more famous neighbours, North Macedonia has a medieval old bazaar, the stunning Lake Ohrid (one of Europe’s oldest and deepest lakes), and mountain terrain that rivals anywhere in the Balkans.

Best for: History, hiking, budget travel Best time to visit: April to October Getting there: Fly to Skopje

9. Papua New Guinea

Not for the faint-hearted, but for serious adventurers, Papua New Guinea offers tribal cultures, active volcanoes, world-class diving, and dense jungle — with almost no other tourists in sight.

Best for: Cultural immersion, diving, jungle trekking Best time to visit: May to October Getting there: Fly to Port Moresby via Brisbane or Manila

10. Georgia (the Country)

Tucked between the Caucasus mountains and the Black Sea, Georgia has ancient cave cities, incredible wine, some of the best hiking in Europe, and a capital city with a thriving food and art scene. All at Eastern European prices.

Best for: Hiking, food & wine, history Best time to visit: April to June, September to November Getting there: Fly to Tbilisi from Istanbul, Dubai, or several European cities

The bottom line? The world is enormous and most tourists see only a tiny, well-worn slice of it. These destinations prove that going a little further off the beaten path rewards you with bigger experiences, lower costs, and stories worth telling.
